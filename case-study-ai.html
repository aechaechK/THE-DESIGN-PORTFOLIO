AI as a Design Collaborator

I use AI to move faster through the parts of the process that don't require human judgment — so I can spend more time on the parts that do. AI handles the first draft; I handle the decisions.

01 — Philosophy

The role AI plays in my work

AI doesn't replace design judgment — it compresses the time it takes to get to a decision worth making. The parts of design work that feel like logistics: first drafts, pattern recognition across large sets of data, writing documentation, generating variants — these are where AI pays off. The parts that require context, taste, and understanding of what a user actually needs still require a human.

The way I think about it: I'm not faster because AI does the work. I'm faster because AI handles the work that was slowing me down from getting to the real work.

"AI handles the first draft; I handle the decisions. That's the only division of labour that makes sense."
— How I frame it when asked about AI in design

03 — Where AI doesn't help

What stays entirely human

Being clear about where AI falls short is as important as knowing where it helps. These are the parts of the design process where I've tried using AI and found it either unreliable or actively counterproductive.

User empathy
AI can summarise what users said. It cannot weigh what they meant against what they need. That gap — between stated preference and actual behaviour — requires a designer who has watched real people struggle with the interface.
Stakeholder navigation
Knowing which decision to push back on, how to frame a design rationale for a particular stakeholder, when to hold the line — none of this can be delegated. Context and relationship are irreplaceable.
Final design judgment
AI generates options. It cannot tell you which one is right for this product, this user, this moment in the roadmap. That call is always the designer's to make.
Design system decisions
AI can document a component. It cannot decide whether a new pattern should be added to the system, consolidated into an existing one, or rejected. Those calls require understanding the full system and its tradeoffs.
04 — Reflection

What this has changed about how I work

The biggest shift isn't speed — it's where I spend creative energy. Before integrating AI into parts of my workflow, a significant portion of design time went into work that felt like logistics: first drafts, formatting documentation, generating enough variants to have a real conversation with a stakeholder. That time now goes elsewhere.

The risk is dependency. I try to stay conscious of which parts of the process I'm delegating and whether I'm losing fluency in anything important. The answer so far is no — the skills that matter are getting more use, not less, because AI is clearing the path to them faster.

The catch: AI tools change fast. The specific tools and workflows I'm describing here reflect how I work now. Some of them will be obsolete or surpassed in six months. What won't change is the underlying principle — use what helps you get to better design thinking faster, and hold onto the parts that require you to be human.
