Team Management

Leading three junior designers through structure, clarity, and creative reflection

My Role
Lead Designer
Team
3 junior designers
Context
First time formally leading a team
01 — Overview

Taking on a team for the first time

I was assigned a team of three junior designers — this was my first time formally leading a team. Alongside my own design responsibilities, I was responsible for guiding them, assigning tasks, and ensuring smooth progress across modules.

It wasn't just about managing workload. It was about creating the conditions for the team to do their best work — with clarity, ownership, and confidence.

02 — The Early Phase

Learning through mistakes

In the beginning, I made a few important mistakes:

  • I did not set up an organised workspace from the start, which caused confusion around timelines and task ownership
  • Feedback was not structured or scheduled, causing it to accumulate and become overwhelming when addressed all at once
  • I struggled to communicate requirements in simple, actionable language. When I worked independently, most decisions stayed in my head — but leading a team made me realise that internal clarity doesn't always translate to shared clarity, which sometimes slowed decisions and caused confusion
  • Many processes were reactive instead of structured

The first few months had friction, but they helped me understand how important clarity, documentation, and simple communication are in team management.

03 — Bringing Structure & Clarity

Building better systems

After recognising these gaps, I started building better systems:

  • We introduced daily morning calls to discuss ongoing work and assign clear priorities
  • I encouraged team members to reach out whenever they had doubts instead of waiting
  • I created a shared Notion workspace to track timelines, assignments, blockers, and updates in one place
  • I began clearly expressing my thought process, documenting key decisions, and openly discussing them with the team before finalising direction to ensure alignment and confidence
  • I introduced more regular and structured feedback conversations
The shift
Over time, this reduced confusion, improved ownership, and made collaboration smoother.
04 — Creative Reflection Sessions

Making space beyond the task

Alongside structured work, we held informal brainstorming and reflection sessions. These were not typical design reviews. Instead, we discussed:

  • Interesting design details we observed that week
  • Products or interfaces that stood out to us
  • Articles that sparked new thinking
  • Film scenes or cinematography choices and how they influenced visual storytelling

We would ask questions like:

  • Why did this design work?
  • What made this interaction memorable?
  • Did observing this change how you approach your own designs?

These sessions helped the team think beyond assigned tasks and develop stronger design awareness. It encouraged curiosity, independent thinking, and creative confidence.

05 — Reflection

What I took away

Although the initial phase was challenging, my teammates were extremely kind and patient during the transition. Their support allowed us to improve the process together.

What I learned: Leadership isn't about being perfect at the start. It's about improving, communicating clearly, and building systems that help the team grow.
