Case Study 01

Improving decision speed through workflow restructuring.

Showcasing the sourcing module redesign

Product
SaaS / Enterprise Procurement
My Role
Lead UX Designer
Users
200+ daily across 3 roles
01 — Overview

Designing for a complex ecosystem

The platform operates within a highly interconnected system of users, workflows, and dependent modules. I led the redesign as lead designer — working closely with a product manager, the operations team, and the development team from research through to handoff.

The challenge was not just simplifying the interface, but doing so without breaking established mental models — ensuring continuity, trust, and ease of adoption across a live client base.

02 — The Problem

A workflow that felt harder than it was

The Sourcing module lets users create RFX requests, invite suppliers, collect pricing, and evaluate responses. The actions were simple — largely form-based — but the experience felt fragmented and mentally taxing due to scattered steps and poorly connected screens.

The business impact was direct: slow client adoption and high support overhead during onboarding. My hypothesis going in was that the module had a significant information hierarchy problem — too much data at equal weight, with no clear signal about what mattered most at each step.

Core insight
Users had no reliable sense of status — they couldn't tell where a request was, who had acted, or what was expected next. This reframed the brief: the problem wasn't reducing steps, it was making progress and ownership visible throughout the flow.
03 — Research

Grounding decisions in real usage

Research was conducted through observation — not formal interviews. Insights came from operations team sessions walking clients through the workflow, and product onboarding calls with new clients. This gave direct visibility into where users hesitated, asked for help, and built workarounds.

The most significant finding was that users had no reliable sense of status — they couldn't tell where a request was, who had acted, or what was expected next. Findings were validated through cross-functional reviews with product, support, and development.

04 — Design Decisions

The specific calls made and why

The approach was to reduce cognitive load by surfacing only what each user needed at each moment — restructuring the flow to feel continuous and role-aware.

Progress visibility — persistent stage indicator vs. breadcrumbs
Chosen
Considered
Breadcrumb navigation — familiar pattern, low engineering effort, already used elsewhere in the product.
Chose instead
Persistent 5-step linear indicator locked to the top of every screen — always visible, shows completed and upcoming stages simultaneously.
Trade-off: Higher engineering cost. Accepted because breadcrumbs only show where you are — they don't show what comes next or signal ownership handoff.
Role-specific views — scoped tabs vs. permission-based visibility
Chosen
Considered
Permission-based visibility — hide fields based on role in the database. Simpler from a UI perspective, consistent page structure across all users.
Chose instead
Role-scoped tab structure — evaluators land directly on the Evaluation Form, irrelevant tabs are deprioritised rather than hidden, preserving discoverability.
Trade-off: More complex tab logic. Justified because hard-hiding content created confusion when users needed to cross-reference — soft-deprioritising worked better in testing.
Communication — inline contextual messaging vs. external email
Chosen
Considered
Continue routing supplier communication through email — familiar to both internal users and external suppliers, no new surface to build or maintain.
Chose instead
Inline contextual messaging tied to the RFX — all communication anchored to the event it belongs to, no external thread tracking required.
Trade-off: Requires suppliers to engage within the platform rather than email. Mitigated by surfacing a clear email notification that links directly to the thread.
05 — Key Screens

Design decisions in context

Screens are intentionally obscured to respect confidentiality. Each annotation maps directly to a decision above. I'm happy to walk through the full unobscured screens during a conversation.

Screen 01 RFX Creation — step-by-step form

The RFX creation flow restructured from scattered pages into a single grouped, multi-step form with persistent progress tracking.

5-step linear flow — users always know their position and what comes next
All core RFX fields consolidated into one grouped section — previously scattered across separate pages
Approver assignment happens at creation — eliminates a separate workflow step that caused delays
Document upload inline with creation — previously required navigating to a separate module
Persistent save at every step — progress is never lost if the user navigates away
Screen 02 Evaluator View — role-scoped interface

The evaluator interface surfaces only what's relevant to evaluation — reducing cognitive load and preventing cross-role confusion.

Live RFX summary — key metrics surfaced at the top so evaluators never have to dig for context
Multi-round visibility — evaluators can track changes and responses across negotiation rounds in one place
At-a-glance stage status — shows what's complete, what's in progress, and how much time remains
Role-scoped tab structure — evaluators land on Evaluation Form directly, irrelevant tabs are deprioritised
Contextual messaging inline — all communication tied to the RFX, no external email threads needed
Screen 03 Comparison Sheet — side-by-side supplier evaluation

The comparison sheet replaces the manual spreadsheet workaround that teams had built outside the platform.

Evaluation mode switching — compare suppliers on technical or commercial criteria independently
Auto-ranked columns — suppliers ordered by best offer, position updates as responses come in
Pin a supplier for focused comparison — designed for multi-round negotiation
Fixed criteria rows aligned across all suppliers — same structure replaces the manual spreadsheet workaround
Screen 04 Status Log — full activity and approval tracking

The status log gives all stakeholders a single source of truth — every action, every decision, in chronological order.

Full activity timeline — every action, by whom, and when, in a single chronological view
Live approval progress — who has reviewed and who is still pending, visible at a glance without chasing anyone
Support requests tracked inline — turnaround time, priority and risk level tied directly to the activity
Flagged line items surfaced in context — reviewers can raise issues without leaving the flow
Remarks captured inline — all feedback tied to the specific RFX, not scattered across emails
06 — Outcomes

What the redesign delivered

The redesign was signed off by enterprise clients and moved into development without a full revision round — which, in a live enterprise product with multiple stakeholder groups, is a meaningful outcome in itself.

The sourcing module went from a flow where users regularly lost track of their position and ownership to one where every step surfaces exactly what a user needs to act on next. The role-specific views, persistent progress indicator, and inline communication reduced the need for teams to rely on external tools and workarounds to manage the process.

I'm not in a position to share specific metrics — I don't have access to post-launch data. What I can speak to is the quality of the design decisions, the clarity of the handoff, and the reasoning behind every call made throughout the process.

If I could go back: I would have pushed for direct observation sessions with evaluators and approvers before finalising the role-specific flows. The designs were validated through stakeholder reviews, but seeing real users navigate the new structure would have surfaced edge cases earlier.
